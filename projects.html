DM TELEVISION

This is a gravitational wave (GW) detector which uses optically levitated microparticles as resonant sensors for sensing small variations in the spacetime curvature. Previously, LIGO has detected these variations in spacetime occurring at frequencies from 10 Hz to few kHz. Those “ripples” in spacetime were generated typically by two compact objects (mostly blackholes, a few neutron stars) inspiraling around each other and eventually merging. The LISA observatory will look for similar GWs at lower frequencies. We aim to look for GWs at higher frequencies, from ten kilohertz to a few hundred kilohertz.

The UC Davis detector is part of a two-node observatory network, with the other node at Northwestern University.

ARIADNE Experiment

The Axion Resonant InterAction DetectioN Experiment (ARIADNE) looks for the QCD axion mediating a spin-dependent force between a mass and a spin. The QCD axion is one of the leading candidates for dark matter , primarily because it also solves an unrelated problem in particle physics, the Strong CP problem.

ARIADNE is searching for a new force mediated by the QCD axion. This force couples a spin and mass via an effective magnetic field depending on the separation between the mass and spin. Therefore, a periodic change in the separation will cause a modulation of this effective magnetic field. This modulation can be measured via NMR; i.e. preparing the spin in a constant magnetic field and modulating the effective field in a transverse direction perpendicular to the bias field. We use a tungsten rotor as the source mass, and hyperpolarized 3He gas as a spin ensemble in this experiment. As the tungsten source mass rotates, the mass (and hence the magnetic field) seen by the 3He spins gets modulated. If this modulation occurs at the Larmor frequency, the spins acquire a slight transverse magnetization, which we then pick up via a SQUID.
